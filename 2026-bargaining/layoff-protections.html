2026 Bargaining Issue Guide

Layoff protections make job cuts less arbitrary and less harmful.

Strong layoff language protects seniority, transparency, recall rights, and worker stability when universities decide to reduce staffing. It does not stop every layoff, but it makes sure classified workers are not left guessing about the rules while their jobs, bills, families, and futures are on the line.

Why layoff language matters

Layoff protections are not abstract contract language. They are the difference between a predictable process and a worker finding out that years of service, department knowledge, and a record of showing up do not count for much when a budget decision comes down.

Classified workers build the day-to-day systems that keep OSU running: labs, housing, dining, facilities, offices, student services, research support, and public-facing campus work. When those jobs are cut without clear rules, the impact does not stay on paper. People need time to plan rent or mortgage payments, health care, child care, elder care, transportation, and whether a different location or shift is even workable.

  • Seniority language helps prevent favoritism and recognizes workers who have served the university for years.
  • Displacement and bumping rights can keep experienced workers employed when a position is eliminated.
  • Clear notice, recall, and vacancy rights give workers time to ask questions, seek representation, and make informed choices.
  • Predictable rules protect institutional knowledge instead of treating workers as interchangeable line items.

What management is trying to weaken

Universities say they face financial pressure and need flexibility. Workers understand budget pressure. Many classified staff live with it every month. But flexibility for management can mean uncertainty for workers if it removes seniority, displacement rights, and predictable pathways into open jobs.

In bargaining, management has proposed eliminating displacement and bumping, arguing that the process is disruptive and often not used. They have also proposed a 60-day qualification period for vacant positions, consideration for vacancies across geographic locations, and a two-year recall list.

Those details matter. A right that is not used every day can still be important because it exists when a worker needs it most. A vacancy across another location may look reasonable in a proposal, but for a worker it can mean a longer commute, a family schedule that no longer works, or a job that is technically available but not realistically accessible. A qualification window can also decide whether someone gets a fair chance to stay employed or is pushed out before they can show they can do the work.

Our union position is straightforward: if the university is going to eliminate jobs, the contract must preserve enforceable layoff protections, seniority, recall rights, and a fair process before people lose their livelihood.

How members can help

Share concrete examples of how layoff uncertainty affects your work area, your family, and the services students and departments rely on. Bargaining is stronger when management hears what these protections mean in real life: time to plan bills, a fair shot at open positions, limits on favoritism, and respect for the knowledge classified workers have built over years on the job.

Visible member support helps our bargaining team defend rights that matter before a crisis hits. Layoff protections are easiest to weaken when people think they will never need them. They are hardest to ignore when members explain why a fair process matters to every worker, even workers who feel secure today.

Quick questions

What are bumping and displacement rights?

They are contract rights that can let a senior worker move into another available or less-senior position instead of losing employment outright when their job is eliminated. The details matter, but the purpose is fair process and job stability.

Why keep a right that is not used every day?

Layoff rights matter most during a crisis. Even when they are not used often, they shape management's decisions and give workers enforceable options before a job loss becomes final.

Does flexibility solve layoffs?

Flexibility for management can become uncertainty for workers if seniority, recall, and displacement rights are weakened. Fair rules protect people from being treated as interchangeable when budgets tighten.
