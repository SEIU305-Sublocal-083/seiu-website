2026 Bargaining Issue Guide

Contracting-out protections keep public university work accountable.

When campus work is moved to outside contractors, OSU risks losing the people who know the buildings, safety routines, students, departments, and day-to-day problems that keep the university running.

Read the Bargaining HubRead They Say / We Say

Why this issue matters

Contracting-out is not an abstract policy fight. It can show up as a vacant classified position replaced by a vendor, a temporary outside contract that becomes the new normal, or campus work being moved away from the workers who understand how OSU actually operates.

Classified workers carry institutional knowledge that does not fit neatly into a bid sheet: which building has recurring issues, which service routes protect student access, where safety concerns come up, and how to solve problems without sending students, faculty, or departments through a maze. When that work is outsourced, the campus can lose continuity, accountability, and stable public jobs at the same time.

What management is proposing

Universities say they need more flexibility. In subcontracting, management has proposed removing obstacles tied to conducting and preparing a feasibility study before contracting-out begins. Their proposal would have the university identify possible cost savings, service quality, or other benefits, then give the union at least 30 days to offer an alternative proposal.

That may sound reasonable until workers look at what would be removed. The feasibility study is not red tape to classified staff. It is a checkpoint before public university work is shifted to a private contractor. It forces the employer to slow down, show its work, and compare outsourcing against a real in-house option instead of treating vendor bids as the obvious answer.

Management has also proposed removing language that requires a contractor to hire displaced workers subject to just cause and continuation of PEBB healthcare. That matters because contracting-out can turn a stable public job into a disruption for the worker, their family, and the campus services they support.

What our union is fighting for

Our position is straightforward: keep the current protections and the enforceable checks before classified work is outsourced. If OSU claims contracting-out will save money or improve service, the university should be willing to prove it before workers are displaced and before campus departments lose experienced staff.

Flexibility should not mean skipping accountability. Classified workers are asking for a fair process that looks at the real cost of outsourcing, including quality, safety, continuity, response time, and the public jobs that make OSU work.

What members can watch for

  • Departments replacing vacant classified positions with vendors instead of hiring into the bargaining unit.
  • Temporary contracting arrangements that quietly become permanent outsourcing.
  • Work leaving campus crews, shops, offices, or service teams without a clear explanation.
  • Claims of savings that do not account for quality, safety, continuity, or the cost of losing experienced workers.
