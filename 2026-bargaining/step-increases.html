2026 Bargaining Issue Guide

Step increases recognize service, experience, and skill.

For classified staff, step movement is not a perk. It is the pay system recognizing that workers become faster, safer, more reliable, and more useful to OSU as they learn their jobs and stay in them.

Why step movement matters

Step increases create predictable progress within a classification. A worker who stays in the job learns OSU systems, campus buildings, safety procedures, student-facing routines, software, purchasing rules, equipment, seasonal deadlines, and the people needed to get work done. That experience has value.

Steps are one way the contract recognizes that value without forcing every worker to change jobs just to move forward. They help departments retain people who know how the work actually happens and help workers plan for rent, groceries, childcare, transportation, medical costs, and staying in public service work.

  • Step movement is separate from a cost-of-living adjustment. COLA helps wages keep up with prices; steps recognize growth and retention within a classification.
  • Workers can lose ground when either step movement or COLAs are frozen, especially when everyday costs keep rising.
  • A long step freeze makes experienced classified staff easier to lose and harder to replace.

What management is saying

The universities are telling the public they face significant financial constraints and need to preserve academic operations, student access, and institutional sustainability. They estimate the union wage proposal would cost $239.4 million through FY31.

They also describe the union's 2026 wage proposal as a 6.50% base increase plus average step movement of 4.62% for eligible employees, which they combine into an 11.12% figure. For 2027, they describe a minimum 4.0% base increase plus average step movement, or 8.62% for eligible employees. Their own proposal would leave steps and base salary increases unchanged through June 30, 2028, with an economic reopener in early 2028.

What that leaves out

Combining step movement with across-the-board raises can make normal wage progression sound like an extra bonus. It is not. Step movement is built into the wage scale because a classified worker usually contributes more after learning the job, the department, the systems, and the common problems that come up.

That matters in real work. A more experienced worker may finish a repair faster, notice a safety issue earlier, handle a difficult student or public interaction more calmly, train a newer employee, prevent mistakes in records or payroll, or keep a service running when staffing is tight. OSU benefits from that experience every day.

When management asks workers to wait years for economic movement, the impact is not abstract. It means workers are asked to keep taking on responsibility, solving problems, and training others while their pay progression stalls.

What our union is fighting for

Our union is fighting to protect step movement and win a wage package that values the classified workers who keep OSU running every day. We can recognize budget pressure without accepting the idea that staff experience should be treated as optional or unaffordable.

Protecting steps is about retention, fairness, and basic respect for work that gets better with experience. If OSU wants dependable services, safe workplaces, and knowledgeable staff, the pay system has to support workers staying and growing in those jobs.

How to talk about it

Ask coworkers what they know now that they did not know on their first day. Ask what breaks when experienced people leave. Ask what a pause in wage progression would mean for rent, childcare, transportation, and whether someone can afford to keep doing public university work.

That is the plain case for steps: workers get better at the job, OSU depends on that growth, and the contract should keep recognizing it.
