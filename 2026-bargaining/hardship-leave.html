Hardship Leave helps coworkers bridge the gap when life does not fit the forms.

Hardship Leave lets OSU classified workers voluntarily donate leave to a coworker facing serious illness, recovery, or family caregiving needs. It is a practical contract safety net for the moments when paperwork, timelines, and real life do not line up neatly.

Why this benefit matters

Classified workers keep OSU running in shops, labs, kitchens, clinics, offices, residence halls, farms, and classrooms. Most of us do not have months of paid leave sitting around for a sudden diagnosis, surgery, injury, pregnancy complication, or family crisis. Rent, groceries, transportation, prescriptions, and childcare do not pause because a claim is pending or a doctor has not signed the right form yet.

Hardship Leave exists because even good leave programs can leave gaps. FMLA can protect a job, but it does not by itself replace a paycheck. Paid Leave Oregon can help, but timing, eligibility, partial wage replacement, documentation, and processing delays can still leave a worker short. Hardship Leave gives coworkers a contract-based way to step in when a member is seriously ill, recovering, or caring for family and the official systems do not cover the whole real-life situation.

What management's proposal would do

Management has proposed eliminating Hardship Leave and has framed it as inequitable or like a popularity contest. That framing misses the basic fact: this is voluntary donated leave from workers who have earned it. It is not a new wage benefit the university has to fund from scratch, and extra donated time can go into a hardship leave pool for eligible workers.

The university says it is trying to protect financial sustainability and preserve benefits. Taking away a low-cost safety net from classified workers does the opposite. It removes one of the few tools coworkers have to help each other through a serious medical or caregiving emergency without forcing someone straight into unpaid leave.

What our union is fighting for

  • Keep Hardship Leave available for OSU classified workers facing serious medical, recovery, or caregiving need.
  • Protect the right of coworkers to voluntarily donate leave and support each other through the contract.
  • Preserve a hardship leave pool so donated time can help eligible workers who may not have a large personal network at work.
  • Reject the idea that FMLA and Paid Leave Oregon make every other safety net unnecessary.
  • Defend a benefit that costs the institution little in direct wage benefit but can keep a worker's household stable during a crisis.
