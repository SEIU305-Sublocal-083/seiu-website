2026 Bargaining Issue Guide

Overtime protections keep extra work from becoming cheaper labor.

OSU classified staff step in when work runs past the schedule: short staffing, emergencies, events, move-in and move-out, labs, facilities, dining, custodial needs, public safety coverage, and administrative deadlines. If management changes how overtime is counted, the work does not get easier. It just gets paid less.

Why overtime matters

Overtime is not an abstract payroll rule. It shows up when a building problem has to be handled after hours, when a campus event needs coverage, when a lab or facility cannot wait until Monday, when dining and custodial crews are stretched, or when administrative deadlines land on top of an already full workload.

In those moments, classified staff are often the people who keep the university running. Overtime protections make sure that when workers give up evenings, weekends, rest time, or time with family, the university pays for that extra labor clearly and fairly.

What is at stake in bargaining

The universities have framed their proposals around financial sustainability and flexibility. Our concern is straightforward: flexibility should not mean making the same extra work cheaper by changing overtime calculations.

Public bargaining updates from the university side say the union's May 28-29 package kept current language on overtime and did not accept OPU's proposed changes. That is the point. SEIU members are working to preserve overtime language that workers can read, understand, and enforce.

Local bargaining updates have warned that management wants to change overtime calculations so workers earn less. If overtime is watered down, the impact is practical: fewer dollars on a paycheck for the same disruption to a worker's life.

Why clear language exists

Clear overtime language prevents confusion before it turns into a paycheck problem. Workers should not have to guess whether a shift extension, callback, schedule change, holiday week, or urgent assignment will be counted correctly.

Strong language also protects departments from solving staffing problems by quietly leaning harder on the same workers. If OSU needs extra work done, the answer should be honest staffing and fair pay, not a rule change that makes overtime easier to avoid.

Member action

If overtime changes would affect your job, talk with a steward or bargaining contact about what happens in practice. Useful examples include forced or expected extra hours, callbacks, event coverage, missed rest time, confusing payroll calculations, or overtime that only happens because a position is vacant.

Real workplace examples help coworkers see what is at stake and help the bargaining team push back with the plain facts of how OSU work actually gets done.
