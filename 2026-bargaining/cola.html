2026 Bargaining Issue Guide

OSU classified staff need COLA raises that keep up with real costs.

A cost-of-living adjustment is not a bonus. It is how wages keep pace when rent, groceries, childcare, transportation, and healthcare costs rise while the work still has to get done.

Read the Bargaining Hub See the 2025 COLA Update

What is at stake

Management's current 2026 bargaining position would leave steps and base salary increases unchanged through June 30, 2028, with an economic reopener in early 2028. For classified workers, that means everyday price increases keep moving while pay does not.

The universities say they face significant financial constraints and need to preserve operations, student access, and long-term sustainability. Those are serious topics. But calling raises expensive does not make a wage freeze harmless. It moves the pressure from university budgets onto workers' household budgets.

  • COLAs protect base wages from being worn down by inflation year after year.
  • When COLAs disappear, workers still have to cover rent increases, groceries, childcare, gas, bus fare, car repairs, and medical costs.
  • Strong COLAs help OSU recruit and retain the skilled staff who keep labs, buildings, dining, custodial services, administrative work, student support, and campus safety functions running.
  • A freeze does not freeze prices. It asks classified workers to do the same essential work while falling behind in real purchasing power.

What our union is fighting for

Our union is fighting for economic language that recognizes the actual cost of living for public university workers across Oregon. COLA language exists because workers should not have to reopen the whole contract every time prices jump. It creates a clear way for wages to respond when the cost of living changes.

The universities describe the union's 2026 wage proposal as the CPI-W average plus 3.0%, totaling 6.50%, with average step movement of 4.62% for eligible employees. They describe that as a combined 11.12% increase for eligible employees in 2026. For 2027, they describe the union proposal as CPI-W plus 2.0%, with a 4.0% minimum, plus average step movement, or 8.62% for eligible employees.

Those numbers sound large when they are presented only as a cost to the employer. For workers, they are about whether a classified staff job can still support a stable life in the communities around OSU. COLAs work with step movement, longevity pay, and benefits protection so workers are not choosing between staying in public service and keeping up at home.

How members can help

Talk with coworkers about what a wage freeze would mean in your workplace and in your household. The strongest argument is often plain: classified workers keep the university running, and our wages need to keep up with the real costs of living near the university we serve.

Wear purple, attend actions, and use the bargaining hub to keep coworkers connected to current updates. When members show that COLA is about retention, stability, and respect for the work, it is harder for management to reduce the issue to a single price tag.

Quick questions

Is a COLA the same thing as a step increase?

No. A COLA helps base wages keep pace with prices. A step increase recognizes service, experience, and progression within a classification. Workers can fall behind when either one is frozen.

Does management's budget concern make a wage freeze harmless?

No. Budget pressure is real, but a freeze shifts the pressure onto classified workers' household budgets. Prices keep moving even when the wage scale does not.

How does this affect OSU services?

When real wages fall behind, OSU has a harder time keeping experienced staff in the jobs students, faculty, researchers, and departments rely on every day.
