Why workers may choose to strike

A strike can move bargaining when other pressure has not.

A strike is a coordinated decision to stop work so management has a stronger reason to reach a fair agreement.

Reviewed: July 20, 2026.

Concern about a strike is reasonable.

Lost pay, student disruption, workload after return and uncertainty are real. Our organizing should answer those concerns with facts, preparation and respect.

The practical case

Why bargaining can reach a strike

Collective bargaining has power because management and workers each face consequences if no agreement is reached. Management can delay or hold its position through mediation. After our union completes Oregon’s public employee bargaining process and any contractual no-strike restriction no longer applies, strike-permitted workers can choose to stop work together. Without a credible ability to say no, bargaining can become a request instead of a negotiation. [1]

A strike is not disruption for its own sake. It makes the cost of refusing a fair settlement visible enough that reaching an agreement becomes preferable. That works best when participation is broad, the demands are understandable and the action remains disciplined.

Reasonable questions before supporting a strike

What exactly are we trying to win?

Our bargaining updates should identify the priority issues, explain management’s proposals and show what our bargaining team is working to win.

What bargaining steps remain?

Members should consider a strike alongside mediation, public final offers, political pressure and other escalation—not as an automatic first move.

How will we support lower-paid coworkers?

Our readiness work should include unemployment guidance, hardship support, food access, transportation and clear expectations about duration.

How do members decide what happens next?

Our union should provide a clear decision process, trusted updates and a way to evaluate a tentative agreement without rumor.

What a strike can and cannot do

It canIt cannot guarantee
Show that OSU depends on classified workers.That every proposal will be won.
Create operational, public and political pressure to settle.A short duration or no financial hardship.
Build shared experience and a stronger member organization.That every coworker will agree or participate.
Change management’s calculation about delay.That relationships return to normal immediately.
Questions make our organizing stronger. Ask what fact or protection would make the choice clearer. A strong strike is built by answering hard questions directly.

Why preparation changes the balance

Management can plan for a dispute with budgets, managers and contingency operations. Our coworkers carry rent, groceries, medications and caregiving obligations. Shared preparation—accurate benefit information, mutual aid, rides, food access and transparent bargaining updates—reduces the pressure on any one person to accept whatever is offered simply because time is running out.

That is also why Oregon’s new limited unemployment coverage matters. It does not eliminate sacrifice, but it can extend the time workers have to make a collective decision. The policy remains contested; supporters and opponents disagree about its effects. The official Oregon Employment Department (OED) page controls eligibility. [2]
