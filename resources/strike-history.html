U.S. and Oregon labor history

How strikes shaped labor law in the U.S. and Oregon.

Workers have used strikes to win recognition, fairer pay, safer workplaces and a voice on the job. The history also includes defeats, state violence, racial exclusion and arguments about the public interest. Oregon’s modern public sector rules grew from those conflicts.

Historical sources reviewed: July 20, 2026.

A note about the numbers

The Bureau of Labor Statistics has work-stoppage data dating to the 1880s, but definitions and coverage changed. Since 1982, its headline “major” series generally counts only stoppages involving 1,000 or more workers, so smaller local strikes are underrepresented.

Selected timeline

From industrial conflict to a regulated right

  1. Late 1800s: national industrial strikesRail, mining and manufacturing disputes made the imbalance between concentrated capital and individual workers visible. Some ended in injunctions, troops, deaths and blacklisting. They also helped make labor law a national political question.
  2. 1935: the National Labor Relations ActThe National Labor Relations Act created a federal framework for many private sector workers to organize and bargain collectively. It excluded public workers, leaving states to develop separate systems.
  3. 1936–1937: Flint sit-down strikeWorkers occupied General Motors plants, and the settlement brought recognition of the United Auto Workers. The action became a prominent example of workers using control over production to force recognition. [2]
  4. 1968: Memphis sanitation strikeBlack sanitation workers struck over safety, pay and union recognition. The campaign connected labor rights and civil rights and drew Martin Luther King Jr. to Memphis.
  5. 1973: Oregon enacts PECBAAfter public sector job actions and debate over how to resolve labor disputes, Oregon adopted the Public Employee Collective Bargaining Act (PECBA). The Oregon Encyclopedia reports that lawmakers concluded a legal, structured right to strike was preferable to unpredictable illegal walkouts. [3]
  6. 1995: Oregon changes the final bargaining stepsThe Legislature replaced mandatory fact-finding with today’s impasse, public final offers and cooling-off structure, while retaining voluntary fact-finding. [4]
  7. 2025–2026: Oregon adds limited unemployment insurance for strikersSenate Bill 916 created limited unemployment eligibility for striking workers beginning in 2026, adding financial support while retaining unpaid weeks and weekly eligibility rules. [5]

What history shows workers can do together

History shows that workers acting together have won union recognition, better pay and hours, safer workplaces and a stronger voice on the job. Those gains came from organizing coworkers, building public understanding, making clear demands and sustaining collective action.

The hopeful and practical lesson is that solidarity can move employers. The strength of any action depends on timing, participation, preparation and workers’ ability to hold together. Oregon’s PECBA framework puts that collective pressure after negotiation, mediation and a cooling-off period.

For this bargaining round: We can learn from past workers while building the power this moment requires. Our current proposals or final offers, legal stage, member vote, strike plan and household supports give members the information needed to decide and act together.
