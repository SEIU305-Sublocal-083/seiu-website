# SEIU Local 503 at Oregon State University - Web Brand Book

This guide is the operational standard for writing, designing, drafting, and publishing Local 083 web content.

## 1) Union Voice Standard

### Core rule
Always speak as **our union**. Never describe the union as a separate outside group.

### Required framing
- Use: "our union," "we," "us," "our members," "our bargaining team."
- Center member agency and collective action.
- Use direct, plain language and clear next steps.

### Do/Don't examples
- Do: "Our union is bargaining for fair wages and stronger workplace protections."
- Do: "We won this because members showed up."
- Don't: "The union is bargaining for employees."
- Don't: "The union says members should attend."

### Voice traits
- Empowering: make clear what members can do now.
- Unified: emphasize solidarity and shared ownership.
- Clear and direct: avoid vague or technical wording when plain wording works.
- Supportive: assume members are busy and need practical guidance.
- Credible: use specific dates, facts, and commitments.

### Page-type voice patterns
- Event pages: practical logistics first, then why attending matters for our union.
- News pages: clear update + impact on members + explicit action.
- Resource pages: rights-first, step-by-step, reusable language.
- About/evergreen pages: who we are, how our union is member-run, how to participate.

### Copy examples
- Headline: "Our Union Bargaining Update: Member Actions This Week"
- CTA: "Join Our Membership Meeting"
- Bargaining update lead: "Our bargaining team met with management on [date] and pushed proposals on wages, workload, and safety."
- Event promo: "Wear purple so management sees our union's strength across campus."
- Spotlight lead: "Meet a member helping build our union at OSU."

## 2) Editorial Style Standard

Use **AP style as the default editorial reference** for grammar, punctuation, capitalization, numerals, dates, and headlines.

Our house rules override AP when needed for union identity, member-facing clarity, or web usability.

Use **Chicago style only as a secondary reference** for formal long-form documents when AP does not provide enough guidance. Chicago is not the default for web copy.

### How to apply this standard
- Website, news, event, and resource pages should follow AP-based rules directly.
- Flyers, emails, and social copy may be looser in length or formatting, but should keep the same union voice and core naming conventions.
- When AP and our union voice conflict, choose the version that keeps our union centered, clear, and direct.

### What AP style means for Local 083
- Use AP as the default for routine writing decisions so pages feel consistent across news, events, and evergreen resources.
- Favor short paragraphs, plain wording, and front-loaded information because most members are scanning on mobile.
- Use AP conventions to reduce friction, then apply Local 083 voice so the copy still sounds like our sublocal rather than a newsroom or HR office.
- If a strict AP choice makes the copy colder, less actionable, or less union-centered, use the house rule instead.

### House rules
- Always write as **our union** or **we**. Do not describe the union as an outside organization speaking about members.
- Prefer plain-language, action-first copy over formal, academic, or institutional phrasing.
- Keep headlines, subheads, buttons, and links concise and direct.
- Use specific dates, locations, and actions when they help members know what to do next.

### Naming and usage conventions
- Union references: prefer "our union," "SEIU Local 503," or "Local 083" as context requires.
- Campus references: use "OSU" for concise references; use "Oregon State University" where full formal naming is needed.
- Bargaining references: prefer "our bargaining team," "bargaining update," and "our contract campaign" over detached institutional phrasing.
- Member references: use "members," "coworkers," or the relevant worker group, not distant labels like "employees" when member language is more accurate.
- Leadership references: use specific role names when possible, such as steward, organizer, executive team, or bargaining team.

### Editorial defaults
- Headlines: use sentence-style clarity and plain wording over clever or formal titles.
- Dates and times: follow AP-style readability, but always favor the clearest member-facing format.
- Numerals and percentages: default to AP treatment unless a campaign, chart, or legal citation needs a different presentation.
- Punctuation and capitalization: default to AP unless a formal title, legal name, or branded usage requires otherwise.

### AP-style writing rules we use most
- **Dates:** use month-day-year wording that is easy to scan. Example: "The rally is April 16, 2026."
- **Times:** use numerals with `a.m.` and `p.m.`. Example: "Join us at 5:30 p.m."
- **Numbers:** use numerals for specific figures, turnout counts, vote totals, wages, percentages, and dates when that is clearest for members.
- **Percentages:** use `%` only in charts, graphics, or campaign materials where space is tight; in body copy, spell out "percent" unless a page format clearly benefits from the symbol.
- **Titles:** lowercase generic role references and capitalize formal titles only when they directly precede a name. Example: "steward Maya Jones" and "Executive Team member Chris Rivera."
- **Headlines:** keep them direct and informative. Prefer "Bargaining update for OSU members" over something abstract or slogan-like.
- **Abbreviations:** spell out unfamiliar terms on first reference when needed, then use the shorter form. Example: "Oregon State University" on first formal reference, then "OSU."
- **Punctuation:** prefer the simplest readable punctuation. Avoid overusing parentheses, semicolons, or quotation marks for emphasis.

### Local 083 examples and overrides
- **Union-first framing override:** AP may tolerate detached institutional phrasing, but our house style does not. Write "Our union filed the demand" instead of "The union filed the demand."
- **Member-centered wording:** write "members" or "coworkers" when that is the real audience. Use "employees" only when the legal or contractual context requires that term.
- **Action-first leads:** start with the outcome or action members need. Example: "Vote by Friday, April 10, to approve our bargaining priorities."
- **OSU context:** name the campus, building, unit, or meeting context when it helps members act quickly. Example: "Meet outside Kerr Administration Building at noon."
- **Campaign language:** use concrete verbs such as "join," "vote," "sign," "attend," "show up," and "contact" rather than vague verbs like "engage" or "participate" when a specific action is known.
- **Sub-local identity:** when a page needs both broader and local identity, introduce "SEIU Local 503" first and then identify "Local 083 at Oregon State University" so members know both the union and the sublocal context.

### Preferred examples
- Do: "Our bargaining team meets with management Tuesday, April 14, and members can join the rally at 11 a.m."
- Do: "SEIU Local 503 members in Local 083 voted to support the proposal."
- Do: "Contact a steward if management calls you into a meeting that could lead to discipline."
- Don't: "The union will be holding an informational event for employees."
- Don't: "Participation is encouraged in order to demonstrate solidarity."
- Don't: "Local 083, which is affiliated with SEIU Local 503, will be convening."

## 3) Content Patterns

Use the existing templates and published pages as the source of truth for content order.

### Event pages
Required order:
- Date label
- Clear event headline
- One-sentence event summary
- Main body with "About this event" context
- Agenda / what to expect section when useful
- Logistics block with date, time, and location
- Primary CTA for registration, Zoom, RSVP, or attendance details

Must include:
- Exact date
- Start time and, when known, end time
- Location or Zoom details
- Clear statement of who should attend
- Concrete next action

Pattern notes:
- Put logistics and CTA above the fold when possible.
- Lead with why the event matters to our union, not only the mechanics.
- CTA text should be specific: "Register here," "Join on Zoom," "RSVP now," or "Add to Calendar."

### News and bargaining updates
Required order:
- Topic label or section kicker
- Direct headline
- Publish date
- Hero image when it adds context
- Lead paragraph with the main update
- Supporting detail sections with descriptive subheads
- Closing CTA tied to the next member action

Must include:
- What changed
- Why it matters for members
- What members should do next
- Relevant dates, deadlines, or vote information

Pattern notes:
- Bargaining posts should foreground wins, open issues, or decisions before background.
- Use short subheads such as "What happened," "Key wins," and "What happens next?"
- End with a CTA even when the update is primarily informational.

### Resource pages
Required order:
- Clear page title
- One-sentence orientation summary
- Most important document, right, or action first
- Supporting cards, sections, or tools grouped by member task
- Steward/contact escalation path where relevant

Must include:
- Clear explanation of what the resource helps a member do
- Direct links to the core document, guide, or contact point
- Plain-language explanation before legal or policy detail

Pattern notes:
- Resources should answer the immediate member question first.
- Put scripts, rights language, and steward contact information in scannable blocks.
- Use cards and short sections instead of long dense text walls when possible.

### Top-level and evergreen pages
Required order:
- Hero kicker or context label when helpful
- Clear headline
- One-sentence value statement
- Primary CTA and optional secondary CTA
- Supporting sections that explain the union, member benefits, or next steps

Must include:
- Clear explanation of who we are or what the page helps members do
- At least one member-facing CTA on high-traffic pages
- Distinct sections with descriptive headings

Pattern notes:
- Homepage and campaign pages should pair the main CTA with one secondary action.
- About-style pages should explain member power, steward support, bargaining, and how to participate.
- FAQs, cards, and repeated section blocks should use consistent heading structure.

### Cross-channel adaptation
- Flyers and emails can compress the section order, but should still preserve headline, summary, key action, and contact path.
- Social posts can reduce the body to one update plus one action, but should use the same naming and CTA standards.

## 5) Visual Assets and Image Use

### Logo and identity use
- Use `/images/logo.png` as the default website logo, favicon, and Apple touch icon unless a page has an approved replacement asset.
- Keep enough clear space around the logo so it does not crowd adjacent text, borders, or other marks.
- For website headers, pair the SEIU 503 wordmark treatment with "Oregon State University" text rather than inventing alternate lockups.
- Use Local 083 wording in page copy and metadata when the sublocal identity adds clarity; keep the logo treatment visually consistent across pages.

### Image file and placement standards
- Store site images in `/images`.
- Prefer `.webp` for content and social images when possible.
- Keep `.png` for the logo, favicon, and other assets that need transparency or existing compatibility.
- Use real production paths in page markup and metadata; do not ship temporary placeholder image URLs.

### Photo and illustration standards
- Prefer authentic images of members, OSU spaces, campaign materials, meetings, or campus life.
- Avoid generic stock imagery that looks disconnected from our members or campus.
- Choose images that reinforce solidarity, action, and member participation.
- If a page does not have a strong contextual image, use the default social card instead of forcing a weak decorative photo.

### Alt text and image treatment
- Alt text should describe the content purpose of the image, not just the file contents.
- Hero images should support the headline and summary, not duplicate them word for word.
- Card images should be readable at smaller sizes and avoid cluttered text baked into the image.
- Flyer or email graphics may use larger headline text in-image, but archived web versions still need page text that stands on its own.

### Social and campaign assets
- Default social card asset: `/images/card.webp`
- Use a custom OG image when a campaign, welcome page, or major announcement has a dedicated visual such as `/images/og-local083-welcome.webp`.
- Reuse approved campaign visuals, such as bargaining graphics and zoom-background art, before creating a new visual direction for a single page.

## 6) Accessibility and Content Design

### Writing and structure rules
- Use descriptive headings that tell members what is in the next section.
- Use meaningful link and button text; do not use "click here," "read more," or other vague labels.
- Write in short paragraphs and scannable chunks for mobile-first reading.
- Put the most important action, right, or update near the top of the page.

### Interface expectations
- Preserve visible focus states on links, buttons, form fields, and disclosure elements.
- Maintain accessible color contrast between text, controls, and backgrounds.
- Use clear heading hierarchy with one `h1` per page and descending section levels.
- Do not rely on color alone to communicate meaning or urgency.

### Content accessibility standards
- Provide meaningful alt text for informative images and empty alt text only for purely decorative images.
- Make CTA text self-explanatory out of context.
- Use tables, lists, and cards only when they improve comprehension; otherwise prefer plain text sections.
- Avoid burying critical details only inside images, PDFs, or social graphics when the page itself can carry the information.

### Quick accessibility checklist
- Headings are descriptive and in order.
- Buttons and links state the action clearly.
- Images have useful alt text.
- The page can be scanned quickly on mobile.
- Focus-visible behavior remains intact.
- Color contrast remains readable.

- Card pattern: white background, rounded corners, border, optional hover shadow

### Button standards
Use the shared button classes and behavior:
- Base: `.btn` for weight, spacing, rounded corners, motion
- Primary: `.btn-primary` for main conversion action
- Secondary: `.btn-secondary` for dark-background alternatives
- Outline: `.btn-outline` for less dominant actions

Behavior expectations:
- Hover: subtle elevation/scale only
- Focus: visible focus ring (`focus-visible` outline)
- Contrast: maintain accessible text/background contrast

## 8) Metadata and Sharing Patterns

Use metadata to match the page's real member purpose, not just the page title.

### Default title patterns
- Event pages: `[Event Name] - SEIU Local 503, OSU`
- News pages: `[Headline] - SEIU Local 503, OSU`
- Resource pages: `[Resource Name] - SEIU Local 503, OSU`
- Top-level pages: `[Page Name] - SEIU Local 503 at Oregon State University`

### Default description patterns
- Event pages: say what the event is, who it is for, and the action or context.
- News pages: summarize the update and why it matters to members.
- Resource pages: explain the right, tool, or document the page provides.
- Top-level pages: explain the union purpose or main member action on the page.

### Social image defaults
- Use `/images/card.webp` as the default `og:image` and `twitter:image`.
- Use a custom image only when the page has a specific campaign visual, member spotlight image, or welcome/share asset that adds real value.
- Keep social images aligned with the page topic and avoid decorative mismatches.

### Summary length and shareability
- Meta descriptions should generally stay around 140-160 characters.
- Social descriptions should be specific, plain-language, and member-facing.
- Avoid generic summaries like "Learn more on our website."

### Page-type sharing rules
- Event pages should surface date or urgency in the title or description when helpful.
- News pages should emphasize the outcome, decision, or member impact.
- Resource pages should emphasize the problem solved or right explained.
- Campaign or welcome pages can use a custom OG image and more motivational summary language if the action remains clear.

## 9) SEO and Publishing Standards (Required)

Production pages must include complete metadata and structured data.

### Required metadata
- `<title>` with page-specific wording
- `<meta name="description">` (target ~140-160 chars)
- Canonical URL (`<link rel="canonical">`) using final production URL
- Open Graph: `og:type`, `og:url`, `og:title`, `og:description`, `og:image`, `og:site_name`
- Twitter: `twitter:card`, `twitter:url`, `twitter:title`, `twitter:description`, `twitter:image`

### Required JSON-LD
- `Organization`
- `WebSite`
- `WebPage`
- `Event` when content is event-specific

### URL and content quality rules
- Canonical URL must match final deployed page path.
- No placeholders, TODOs, or dummy links on publish (`#`, `[LINK]`, etc.).
- Internal links and asset paths must resolve.
- Use meaningful `alt` text on content images.

### Required operational checks before publish
1. Update content index files where applicable:
   - `events/events.json` for events
   - `news/news.json` for news
2. Regenerate sitemap and update robots entry if needed:
   - `bash ./generate_sitemap.sh`
3. Run quality checks:
   - `python scripts/site_quality_check.py --strict-placeholders`
4. Confirm RSS automation expectations:
   - changes to `events/events.json` or `news/news.json` trigger `.github/workflows/rss-feeds.yml` to update:
     - `events/rss.xml`
     - `news/rss.xml`
     - `feed.xml`
